Bushfires in Australia

Introduction

Bushfires have long been a significant environmental and social concern, especially in Australia, a country known for its vast bushlands and arid landscapes. With its unique climate and vegetation, Australia is prone to intense and frequent bushfires, which pose a threat to ecosystems, human lives, and infrastructure. The growing impact of climate change has further heightened the frequency and severity of these fires, making the need for effective monitoring and response strategies more critical than ever. This visualisation is aimed to provide a clear look of bushfires happened in Australia that are detected by NASA satellites around the summer period in 2019.

Distribution of Bushfires

The geographic map illustrates the spatial distribution of bushfires across Australia, highlighting areas affected by varying levels of fire activity. Each circle represents detected bushfires, with the size of the circle indicating the Fire Radiative Power (FRP) in megawatts (MW). Larger circles signify higher FRP, providing insight into the intensity of fire activity at different locations. The heatmap coloring conveys the confidence level (0-100%) of the fire detection, where darker shades represent higher confidence that a fire was present.

Around this period of time, there is significant clustering observed along the eastern coast. The high fire radiative power in certain areas, combined with high confidence values, underscores the severity and prevalence of bushfires in regions prone to hot and dry conditions.

Distribution by Region: Eastern Australia Focus

The bar chart provides an overview of the regional distribution of bushfires across Australia, categorised into the West (longtitude < 130), Middle (130 < longtitude < 145), and East (longtitude> 145). It is evident that the eastern region experiences significantly more bushfires compared to the central and western areas.

The geographic map on the right zooms in on eastern Australia, providing a closer look at the concentration of bushfire events. The East, with its dense vegetation and often dry, hot conditions, creates an environment conducive to the spread of fires, making it the most affected region during the bushfire season. This is proved by the size of the bubbles on the east, as they are tend to be larger and the confidence of each bushfire is high.

Fire Radiative Power, Brightness, and Confidence

The scatter plot illustrates the relationship between Fire Radiative Power (FRP), brightness, and confidence level in bushfire detection. The positive trend seen in the plot suggests that as FRP increases, the brightness also tends to rise, indicating more intense fires. Higher brightness makes it easier for satellites to detect and confirm bushfire events, leading to higher confidence in the accuracy of the recorded data.

When brightness values are around 300, the confidence level tends to be lower. This may be due to the conditions under which the fire is being detected, such as daytime, when background brightness can make it harder for satellites to distinguish fires from their surroundings. Additionally, lower FRP values indicate smaller fires, which are inherently more challenging to detect and accurately confirm. These factors contribute to the variability in confidence levels, emphasizing the limitations of satellite-based detection for less intense or obscured fire events.

Day vs. Night Bushfire Activity Over Time

The stacked bar chart visualises the daily bushfire occurrences over a specific time period, distinguishing between daytime (yellow) and night time (brown) fires. The majority of bushfires are seen during the day, as indicated by the predominance of yellow bars, especially in early September when a sharp increase in fire activity is observed.

The peak in early September aligns with Australia's summer season, characterised by extremely dry and hot conditions that significantly increase the likelihood of fires. The combination of daytime heat and dry vegetation creates a high-risk environment, which is reflected in the increased fire activity during this time.

Conclusion: Understanding Bushfire Dynamics in Australia

The Australia summer brings dry, hot conditions that significantly increase the risk of bushfires. During this period, high temperatures and low humidity create an environment where vegetation becomes extremely dry and highly flammable. Additionally, strong summer winds can spread fires rapidly, turning small ignitions into uncontrollable blazes.
